01

The problem

The thing this app exists to kill.

"Every time I'm working in VS Code, I run a command, and while it executes I open Instagram Reels or YouTube. My brain drains faster, and I lose the thread of what I was doing."

This is context-switch addiction. The dead moments in deep work — a compile, a test run, a moment of being stuck — are exactly when the brain reaches for a dopamine hit. The cost isn't just the minutes lost to scrolling; it's the re-entry tax of rebuilding focus afterward, which can take 15–20 minutes each time.

The problem is real, common, and well-documented. It is worth solving.

02

The idea

A Pomodoro timer fused with a focus-state lock.

A desktop app where you define a focus session — for example, 4 hours — broken into work/break intervals (50 min work, 10 min break). During work blocks, everything is locked except a whitelist of apps and websites you explicitly allow. During break blocks, everything unlocks and a visible clock counts down your rest. When the break ends, work mode resumes automatically.

The defining mechanics

  • Whitelist, not blacklist. You define what's allowed (VS Code, Blender, specific sites) — everything else is blocked by default.
  • Per-session intent. Each session names what you're working on, so the lock adapts to the task (coding vs. studying vs. 3D work).
  • Channel-level web allowlist. Not just "block YouTube" — allow one specific channel (e.g. a lofi stream) while blocking the rest.
  • Freedom as reward. Breaks are fully unlocked. The break clock is large and unavoidable, so rest feels earned, not stolen.

03

Reality check

We grilled the idea against the market and real-world behavior before committing. Here's the honest assessment.

It already exists — and the best ones are ruthless

Cold Turkey, Freedom, and FocusMe already occupy this space. Cold Turkey is the "nuclear option": once a block starts, there's no way out — you can't uninstall, restart, or talk your way past it. Freedom syncs blocklists across Mac, Windows, iOS, Android, and Chrome.

What's missing from them: personal context-awareness (knowing you're in VS Code), per-app focus modes, channel-level allowlists, and — critically — a UI good enough to want on your screen.

DimensionAssessment
Problem validityStrong A real, widespread pain.
Technical feasibilityMedium Blocking is solvable; circumvention-proofing is the hard part.
Market uniquenessContested Cold Turkey + Freedom cover ~80%. Differentiation must come from UX + context-awareness.
The real moatDesign The aesthetic-study niche is underserved by ugly, utilitarian blockers.

The retention trap. Research across 525,000 users found a median of ~70% abandon productivity apps within the first few weeks. The escape hatch is the killer: any easy "pause" button will be used. The app must be firm — one deliberately annoying exit, not a soft off-switch.

The phone gap. A desktop app can't touch your phone — where much doom-scrolling actually happens. Out of scope for v1, but it's the honest limitation and the eventual opportunity (phone pairing).

04

Who it's for

Not "everyone who procrastinates." A specific, reachable crowd.

The aesthetic study crowd

Students, self-learners, indie devs, and creatives who care that their tools look good on screen — the people who post their desk setups, share their study tools, and live in dark mode with lofi playing. They will screenshot a beautiful focus timer and share it. That sharing is the distribution.

Deliberately NOT targeting: the cute/cozy cottagecore aesthetic (mascots, illustrated forests, "good job!" affirmations). The taste here is a serious tool that happens to be beautiful — closer to Linear, Arc, and Things 3 than to Forest or a Notion template.

Positioning shift we made consciously

06

Calm Mode & Command Mode

The same session, the same lock — two personalities. The user picks the one that matches how they work, or how they feel that day.

This came out of a simple observation: a big, ever-present clock makes some people work harder — the visible-progress effect, where tangible, always-on effort drives output. But the same density that motivates one person overwhelms another. So instead of guessing, we let the user choose the surface.

🌙 Calm Mode

One thing at a time. A huge 180px timer, generous empty space, distractions hidden. The zen-study philosophy — nothing on screen but the work and the time. The default.

🛰️ Command Mode

Mission control. The timer sits center stage, flanked by live-ticking stats, today's blocks chart, XP earned, and a focus wall filling in real time. Everything visible at once — the momentum made tangible.

What's in Command Mode

  • Center timer with a count direction toggle — time left (countdown urgency) or focused (count-up reward). User's choice.
  • Live session stats — focused-today as an accumulating clock, day streak, distractions blocked, all ticking in real time.
  • Today's blocks chart — a small bar chart of blocks done vs. planned.
  • XP & level — XP earned this session with the progress bar toward the next level.
  • The focus wall — a 28-day contribution-graph grid where brighter cells mean more focus. "Today is brightest" is the reward you chase.

The floating mini timer (both modes)

An optional always-on-top pill — just the block countdown and a live dot — that sits over VS Code or Blender while you work. This is the piece that's actually visible during deep work, since you won't be looking at any full Lockd screen. It's the glanceable clock from the "wall of monitors" setups, distilled to one honest signal: how long until your break.

The tension to respect. Calm Mode says "hide everything, just focus." Command Mode says "show everything, feel the momentum." They're philosophically opposite — and Command Mode risks becoming a distraction itself (live-ticking stats while you should be heads-down). The design rule: motion in Command Mode stays slow and peripheral; the focus wall and stats update between glances, never demand them.

Why ship both. It resolves the old "calm or vibrant timer?" question by handing it to the user, and it makes a stronger pitch — one app that flexes from monk-mode to mission-control. The cost is real (two full surfaces to design and maintain), so the floating mini timer may end up the most-used piece of all.

07

Feature scope

What's in v1, and what waits.

v1 — core

  • Session setup: duration, work/break rhythm, focus intent
  • Whitelist of allowed apps (select from installed apps)
  • Web allowlist with channel-level granularity (e.g. one YouTube channel)
  • Work-mode lock that blocks everything outside the allowlist
  • Break mode with large, unavoidable countdown
  • Calm Mode & Command Mode work surfaces (user-switchable)
  • Floating always-on-top mini timer with count up/down toggle
  • Focus wall (contribution-graph grid) in Command Mode
  • One deliberately-annoying early exit (not a soft pause)
  • Dashboard with gamified stats
  • Dark + light themes

Gamification (all five, on the dashboard)

🔥 Day streak

Consecutive days with a completed session.

⏱️ Focus this week

Total hours logged, with week-over-week trend.

✅ Sessions today

Completed vs. planned for the day.

🛡️ Distractions blocked

Count of attempts intercepted — the satisfying number.

⚡ Level & XP

XP earned per focus block; levels with names ("Flow Master"). Progress bar toward next level on the dashboard.

Later — v1.1+

  • Phone pairing (the real gap in the market)
  • Context-aware locking (allowlist changes based on the foreground app)
  • History & goals views, schedules
  • Cross-platform (macOS)

Design direction

How we arrived at the look — and the references that shaped it.

We worked through Dribbble references and narrowed taste by what was rejected as much as what was chosen.

ReferenceVerdict & why
Valo (dark blue, soft glow)Liked Atmospheric depth — dark but alive, frosted panels.
Requo (minimal white, editorial)Liked Clinical precision, every pixel earns its place.
Timeyfi (illustrated, cozy greens)Rejected Too cute. No mascots, no forests, no affirmations.
CyberX (purple-navy, data viz)Adopted → Dark High-contrast, gradient depth, energizing.
Noteflow (white, multi-color cards)Adopted → Light Bright, multi-accent, gamified card feel.

The two themes

◐ Dark — "CyberX"

Deep purple-to-navy gradient base. High contrast. Multi-color accent data. Glowing active states. The late-night study mode.

◑ Light — "Noteflow"

Warm off-white. Multi-colored stat cards. Soft borders, no heavy shadows. Bright, energetic, daytime productivity.

The multi-color accent system carries across both themes — so dark mode isn't mono-sage; it gets the full colorful treatment too.

Technical approach

Windows-first, built on Tauri.

Why Tauri over Electron

TauriElectron
Bundle~10 MB150 MB+
Idle RAM~30 MB200–400 MB
System hooksRust backend — strongNode.js — weaker for OS-level work
UIHTML/CSS/JS — your existing skillsSame

The UI is plain web tech (your strength). The Rust parts needed for v1 are mostly copy-paste level — you don't need deep Rust to ship.

How blocking works on Windows

MethodBypass-resistanceDifficulty
Hosts file redirect to 127.0.0.1Low (VPN/DNS)Easy
Firewall rules per-domainMediumMedium
Local proxy intercepts trafficHighHard

For v1, the hosts-file approach is a reasonable start (it's roughly what Cold Turkey does). Channel-level allowlisting for sites like YouTube needs deeper URL matching — likely a companion browser extension or local proxy, which is the harder, later piece.

12

Open questions

Decisions still to make.

  • Name. "Lockd" is a placeholder. Needs a final decision and a domain check.
  • Theme cohesion. Dark and light currently feel like different products. Intentional contrast, or pull them closer?
  • Timer screen energy. Resolved Shipping both — Calm Mode (minimal) and Command Mode (mission-control), user-switchable. See section 06.
  • The exit mechanism. What's the one annoying-enough early exit? (Type a phrase, solve math, timed countdown.)
  • Forced vs. skippable breaks. Should "skip break, resume work" exist, or are breaks mandatory?
  • Monetization. One-time purchase (Cold Turkey model) vs. freemium. MS Store for credibility, not discovery.

The beautiful UI is the marketing asset — it's what gets screenshotted and shared.
